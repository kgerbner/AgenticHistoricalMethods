Transcribing a 17th-Century Letter with Claude

tutorialpaleographyprimary sources

Secretary hand is the bane of many an early modernist. Here's how I used Claude as a first-pass reader — and why I still checked every word.

The setup

I had a high-resolution scan of a 1680s letter. I described the visible text and pasted a rough OCR, then asked Claude for a transcription preserving original spelling, bracketing anything uncertain.

This is a 1680s English manuscript letter, secretary hand.
Transcribe preserving original spelling; bracket uncertain readings;
list expanded abbreviations; flag low-confidence lines explicitly.
[OCR/description pasted here]

What it got right — and wrong

Claude handled the bulk of the prose well and correctly expanded several common abbreviations. But it confidently "read" a smudged place-name that, against the image, was clearly something else — exactly the kind of plausible error you must catch yourself.

The lesson

A first pass is a gift; an unverified transcription is a liability. I corrected against the scan line by line and logged the model's mistakes as part of my methods notes.
